Overview

1 in 7 Adults

More than 35.5 million people, or 14%, are estimated to have CKD.

Undiagnosed CKD

As many as 9 in 10 adults with CKD do not know they have it.

Severe CKD Awareness

About 1 in 3 adults with severe CKD do not know they have it.

Age Factor

CKD is more common in people aged 65 years or older (34%).

Gender Distribution

CKD is slightly more common in women (14%) than men (12%).

Race/Ethnicity

CKD is more common in non-Hispanic Black adults (20%).

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) is a progressive loss of kidney function, often caused by conditions like high blood pressure and diabetes. It can lead to kidney failure, requiring dialysis or transplantation.

Stages of Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

Stage 1

GFR: 90 and higher

Your kidneys are working well but you have signs of mild kidney damage.

Stage 2

GFR: 60 to 89

Your kidneys are working well but you have more signs of mild kidney damage.

Stage 3a

GFR: 45 to 59

Your kidneys aren’t working as well as they should and show mild to moderate damage.

Stage 3b

GFR: 30 to 44

Your kidneys show moderate damage and don’t work as well as they should.

Stage 4

GFR: 15 to 29

You have very poor kidney function; your kidneys are severely damaged.

Stage 5

GFR: Less than 15

Your kidneys are very close to failing or have stopped working.

How is CKD Treated?

While there is no cure for CKD, you can take steps to preserve kidney function:

  • Regular Healthcare Visits: Keep appointments with your healthcare provider or nephrologist.
  • Manage Diabetes: Control your blood glucose levels.
  • Avoid Harmful Medications: Stay away from painkillers that can worsen kidney function.
  • Control Blood Pressure: Keep your levels in check.
  • Follow a Kidney-Friendly Diet: Limit protein, sodium, and potassium intake.
  • Don’t Smoke: Quitting improves overall health.
  • Stay Active: Aim for regular exercise on most days.
  • Maintain a Healthy Weight: Keep your weight within a healthy range.

Medications for Kidney Disease

Your nephrologist may prescribe:

  • ACE Inhibitors or ARBs: To help lower blood pressure.
  • Phosphate Binders: For phosphate elimination.
  • Diuretics: To remove excess fluid.
  • Cholesterol-Lowering Medications: To manage cholesterol levels.
  • Erythropoietin: To build red blood cells if you’re anemic.
  • Vitamin D and Calcitriol: To prevent bone loss.

What is Kidney Dialysis?

In end-stage kidney disease, dialysis may be necessary:

  • Hemodialysis: Blood is filtered through a machine; requires treatments about three times a week.
  • Peritoneal Dialysis: A solution is placed in the abdomen through a catheter; can be done at home.

What is a Kidney Transplant?

A kidney transplant involves replacing a diseased kidney with a healthy one from:

  • Living Donors: Family members or friends.
  • Deceased Donors: Organs from individuals who have consented to donate.

On average, it takes about three to five years for a kidney from a deceased donor.

Prevention of Kidney Disease

To help prevent CKD, consider the following:

  • Regular Check-ups: Visit your healthcare provider regularly.
  • Manage Blood Pressure: Keep it within healthy limits.
  • Control Blood Sugar: Especially if you have diabetes.
  • Eat a Balanced Diet: Focus on nutritious foods.
  • Avoid Tobacco: Don’t smoke or use tobacco products.
  • Stay Active: Engage in at least 30 minutes of physical activity most days.
  • Maintain Healthy Weight: Keep your weight in a healthy range.
  • Use Pain Relievers Carefully: Follow directions to avoid kidney damage.
  • Limit Alcohol: Drink in moderation.
